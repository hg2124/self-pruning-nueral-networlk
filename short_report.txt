In this project, I used an L1 penalty on sigmoid-gated weights.Think of it like a "tax" on every active connection. The Sigmoid function acts as a dimmer switch, turning connections between 0 (off) and 1 (on). By applying an L1 penalty to these switches, we’re essentially telling the network: "Every connection you keep costs you points. If a weight isn't helping you classify the image, turn it off to save your score." Over time, the network learns to "starve" the useless connections until their gates hit zero, leaving only the essential neural pathways.
The Trade-off: Results SummaryAs I increased the Lambda value (the "tax" rate), the network became more aggressive about cutting weights. 
Here is the breakdown of how the model performed:

Gate Distribution AnalysisLooking at the final gate distribution for the best model (lambda = 10^{-4}, the results are fascinating:The "Dead Zone" (0.0): There is a massive spike here. Most of the network's potential connections were discarded because they didn't contribute to the logic.The "Core" (0.8 - 1.0): There is a smaller cluster of gates that stayed fully open. The L1 penalty successfully forced the network to make a "binary" choice: keep it or kill it.4. Final ConclusionThis approach proves that we don't need all the neurons we're given. It's a much more efficient way of designing AI than just guessing the right size from the start.
